# Institution Selection & Rationale

## Selection Criteria
Institutions were chosen to enable three layers of comparison:
1. **Ivy League** (core analysis) — the original scope
2. **Elite Privates** (peer comparison) — similar scale, different governance
3. **Top Public Universities** (structural comparison) — different constraints, transparency, donor bases
4. **Elite Liberal Arts Colleges** (scale comparison) — smaller endowments, higher per-student ratios, different investment constraints

## Why Expand Beyond Ivies?

The Ivy-only analysis answers: "Who performed best among peers?"
The expanded analysis answers: "What institutional characteristics drive endowment success?"

Key analytical questions the expanded set enables:
- Does endowment size create access advantages (can $40B Yale access better GPs than $3B Williams)?
- Do public university governance constraints (state oversight, FOIA, political pressure) hurt returns?
- Do LACs with higher per-student endowments take more or less risk?
- Does the Yale Model work at every scale, or only for mega-endowments?
- How does disclosure quality correlate with performance across institution types?

## Disclosure Landscape by Tier

### What Each Tier Typically Discloses

**Ivies & Elite Privates:**
- 990s: Yes (all file)
- Audited financials: Yes (all publish)
- Detailed asset allocation: Varies widely (Yale excellent, Brown minimal)
- Investment office reports: Some (Yale, Harvard, MIT, Princeton publish; others don't)
- 13F filings: Most file (public equity holdings only)

**Public Universities:**
- 990s: Generally no (state entities), but foundations may file
- Audited financials: Yes, often more detailed due to state requirements
- Asset allocation: Usually good (public accountability)
- Board minutes: Often public (sunshine laws, FOIA)
- Investment office reports: Usually published
- Compensation: Public record in most states

**Liberal Arts Colleges:**
- 990s: Yes
- Audited financials: Yes, but less detailed
- Asset allocation: Minimal (small staff, less reporting infrastructure)
- Investment office reports: Rare (often outsource to OCIO)
- Key difference: Many use Outsourced CIO (OCIO) model — don't manage internally

### Analytical Implications
- Public universities provide BETTER disclosure than privates for many metrics
- LACs often outsource investment management, creating different performance dynamics
- The OCIO vs internal management comparison is itself a finding worth exploring
